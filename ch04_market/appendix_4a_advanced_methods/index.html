    Appendix 4A: Advanced Methods for Market Sizing and Patient Finding

    Chapter 4 sizes the fictional Roventra opportunity with a practical chain: define the market, validate the diagnosis rule, anchor the denominator outside the claims panel, apply age, treatment, access, and conversion assumptions, estimate the patients claims data misses, and turn the hidden count into a ranked list for action.

    The chapter keeps the method set small so the estimate stays auditable. This appendix names the methods that sit behind or beyond that workflow.

    1. Choose the Right Population Clock

    Chapter 4 uses prevalence because Roventra is modeled as a chronic therapy. The diagnosed population is the stock of adults living with the condition at a point in time. That is the right frame for a steady-state market, label expansion, and broad opportunity sizing.

    Some markets need an incidence frame. Incidence counts new cases during a period. Use it when the product is taken once, used for a short episode, or tied to a new diagnosis event. An adjuvant oncology regimen, a curative therapy, or a short-course acute treatment should start from new eligible patients per year, then move through diagnosis, testing, access, treatment, and persistence.

    Analog forecasting is a third frame. The team starts from a comparable launch and adjusts for label, access, competition, channel mix, and time since approval. Analog models are useful early, when the epidemiology estimate is weak or the market has unusual launch dynamics. They work best as a triangulation check alongside a clearly defined patient funnel.

    2. Build Better Phenotypes

    Chapter 4 uses a simple claims phenotype: at least 1 paid launch diagnosis code. The chapter then compares it with a stricter 2-diagnosis rule. That small rule change shows the core tradeoff: broader capture finds more true patients and also admits more false positives.

    Production phenotypes usually combine several evidence types:

    • diagnosis codes, with position, recency, and repeat evidence
    • procedure codes that imply testing, monitoring, or disease management
    • pharmacy fills for launch, competitor, and supportive therapies
    • lab values, such as A1C in the Chapter 4 diabetes-style example
    • enrollment continuity, so missing evidence is not confused with missing disease
    • provider specialty and care setting, when those fields clarify the context

    The rule should match the business question. A broad education program can tolerate more false positives. A targeted rare-disease field program usually needs a cleaner list. A forecast may need both, reported as a range.

    3. Add Unstructured Evidence When Eligibility Depends on It

    Claims data often misses the fields that determine eligibility for modern therapies. Cancer stage, biomarker status, mutation results, ECOG performance status, ejection fraction, and symptom severity often live in notes, pathology reports, radiology text, or scanned documents.

    Natural-language processing and large language models can extract these fields from unstructured clinical text. Treat the extracted field as a measured variable with error. Validate it against a labeled sample, report sensitivity and precision, and decide whether the error changes the commercial action.

    This matters most when the label depends on the field. A broad diagnosis code may identify patients with the disease. The unstructured field identifies who is actually eligible.

    4. Anchor the Denominator Outside the Panel

    Chapter 4 anchors the diagnosed population to NCHS diagnosed diabetes prevalence and the Census adult population. The claims panel then supplies composition and rates: age eligibility, untreated share, and access probability.

    Other markets need different anchors:

    • SEER for cancer incidence, prevalence, stage, and survival
    • CDC WONDER for mortality and natality
    • Global Burden of Disease estimates for cross-national disease burden
    • published epidemiology studies for conditions missing from national surveys
    • registry counts when a disease has a high-quality clinical registry
    • claims-derived prevalence studies when public survey data does not measure the condition well

    The rule is the same as Section 4.4: keep the denominator outside the enriched panel. A panel can describe what patients look like after they enter observable care. It rarely tells you how many people exist in the country.

    5. Transport the Panel When Composition Matters

    The main Chapter 4 estimate uses a direct national anchor and panel rates. That is simple and transparent. In larger studies, the panel may differ from the target population by age, sex, geography, payer, race, care setting, or disease severity. Then the panel rates need transport.

    Post-stratification applies panel rates inside known population cells, then sums the cells to the target population. Multilevel regression and post-stratification, often called MRP, adds a model before that step. It estimates cell-level rates with partial pooling, so sparse cells borrow information from related cells before being applied to Census or epidemiology cell counts.

    Use these methods when the decision depends on subgroup mix. They need a population frame, cell definitions, model checks, and a plain explanation of what changed compared with the unadjusted estimate.

    6. Estimate Patients Both Sources Miss

    Chapter 4 uses the two-source Chapman estimator. Source 1 is the paid launch-diagnosis source. Source 2 is paid Roventra use. The overlap estimates how many launch-condition patients both sources miss.

    The method is useful because it makes the invisible population visible as a count. Its main assumption is also strong: the chance of appearing in the sources should be similar across patients, or the estimator can move in the wrong direction. In real data, patients with more severe disease are more likely to appear in both medical and pharmacy records. That dependence usually makes the hidden population harder to estimate.

    Production extensions include:

    • 3 or more source capture-recapture, using diagnosis, pharmacy, lab, registry, or EHR sources
    • log-linear models that estimate dependence between sources
    • Bayesian models that allow capture probability to vary by patient characteristics
    • sensitivity analyses that show how the estimate changes when source dependence is stronger or weaker

    Use capture-recapture when the hidden population is material to the decision. If the missing group would not change the forecast, access strategy, or targeting plan, a clear caveat may be enough.

    7. Turn a Hidden Count Into a Ranked List

    Chapter 4's patient-finding model uses structured features around medical activity, competitor fills, A1C results, diabetes-like therapy evidence, age, region, and sex. It scores undiagnosed patients and shows that the top decile contains many more true launch-condition patients than the full undiagnosed pool.

    Production patient finding extends that example in 4 ways:

    • richer longitudinal features from diagnosis, procedure, pharmacy, lab, and care-setting histories
    • sequence models or embedding models that learn patterns across ordered events
    • careful leakage control, so the model does not learn the same code that defines the label
    • monitoring after deployment, because care patterns, coding, access, and competitor behavior change

    Patient finding is common in rare disease, under-diagnosis, and specialty markets where the commercial question includes both patient count and likely treating physicians.

    8. Convert Scores Into Compliant Commercial Action

    Chapter 4 turns the scored list into 2 outputs: an HCP targeting table and a de-identified audience seed. That boundary matters. The patient IDs are tokenized, and the analysis file supports aggregate action.

    For HCP action, the model output is aggregated to physicians, accounts, territories, or care settings. The field team gets a provider-level signal, such as high-scoring undiagnosed patient concentration. The message should stay clinical, educational, and provider-level.

    For direct-to-consumer advertising in markets where it is allowed, the high-scoring patients can become a seed audience inside a privacy-preserving clean room. Token exchange vendors and data partners can match de-identified health data to advertising audiences without exposing identities to the brand. The output is an audience strategy for approved channels.

    Every activation path needs legal, privacy, medical, and compliance review. The model score is only one input to an approved workflow.

    9. Model Timing When a Static Funnel Is Too Coarse

    Chapter 4 uses a static funnel because it teaches the market-sizing bridge clearly. Some markets need time. Patients progress, switch, discontinue, restart, lose coverage, gain coverage, and move through lines of therapy.

    Microsimulation models individual patients over time. They can represent diagnosis delay, treatment start, persistence, switching, progression, and death. Agent-based models add interaction between patients, physicians, payers, accounts, and supply constraints. Health economics models often use this structure when disease progression and treatment sequencing drive value.

    Use a time model when timing changes the answer. If the decision only needs the current untreated opportunity, the static funnel is easier to audit and explain.

    10. Make Uncertainty Useful

    An advanced market-sizing model should show which assumptions move the decision.

    Common uncertainty layers include:

    • parameter uncertainty, such as access probability or conversion rate
    • measurement uncertainty, such as diagnosis-rule sensitivity and precision
    • source uncertainty, such as claims panel coverage or lab completeness
    • structural uncertainty, such as 1-diagnosis versus 2-diagnosis phenotypes
    • scenario uncertainty, such as optimistic and conservative access assumptions

    The decision brief should separate measured data, external estimates, and scenario assumptions. That is the practical discipline behind the Chapter 4 bridge from 29.2 million diagnosed patients to 1.0 million expected starts.
